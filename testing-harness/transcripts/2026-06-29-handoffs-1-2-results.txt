Cowork: "Honestly? The execution is excellent and the design is good-but-modest — and the most interesting thing about it is a tension it doesn't quite resolve.

The standout is restraint. The linter refuses to make semantic judgments: `do-not-needs-invariant` checks only that a field exists, and you declined `guardrail-unenforced` because it would nag a legitimate majority. Holding that line — "the linter flags structure, the grammar carries quality" — is the hardest discipline in a tool like this and the easiest to rationalize away in the name of being helpful. That it held across three handoffs is the best signal here.

The three changes also form a real ladder of guardrail strength: enumerate instances → state the general invariant → bind it with a test. That's a coherent escalation, not three bolt-ons.

Now the tension I'd actually push on. The failure that motivated all of this was a *reader* problem — a weak model read past a clear guardrail. But two of the three changes are *author* improvements: `invariant` makes the prose more general, and `do-not-needs-invariant` checks the author wrote a field. Better prose is still prose, and a model that reads past a clear guardrail reads past a well-generalized one too. The only change that actually addresses the runtime failure is `enforced-by`, because a failing test binds whether or not the comment was honored. And `enforced-by` is the one piece with *no* enforcement — a recommended convention, a forward-reference nobody checks resolves, passes, or even tests the right thing. So the strongest medicine is the least required, and the most-enforced finding targets the weakest lever. That's a defensible set of choices (you genuinely can't require a test to exist), but be clear-eyed that the system's answer to "models ignore guardrails" is still mostly "write better, and optionally bind it."

Two smaller things I'd watch. First, `invariant` can decay into ceremony: the cheapest way to silence the linter is an invariant line that just paraphrases the do-not, and now you've trained authors to cargo-cult a field. The mitigation is purely cultural. Second, accumulation — this is the third guardrail field in a row, and a guardrail that "needs" why + do-not + invariant + enforced-by (+ relocation when external-only) is getting heavy. Ceremony is exactly what makes people write the block lazily or skip it, which would be self-defeating. Individually each field earns its place; collectively, watch the weight.

And the meta-point I raised before still stands: this is all reasoned-forward from anecdotes onto an unverified foundation. We keep refining guardrail *authoring* while the question of whether agents follow any of this — the battery in FUTURE.md §3 — stays unrun. The quiet vindication is that `enforced-by` is the one move that doesn't depend on that assumption at all. If the battery ever comes back "agents don't reliably honor annotations," `enforced-by` is the part that survives, and the invariant-authoring layer is the part you'd have to justify on weaker grounds. I'd run the battery before adding a fourth field."

Me: "How would you resolve tensions in these cases?"

Cowork: "Most of them I'd resolve by *not building* — and being honest about why. Only one is a real deferred feature. Here's how I'd take each.

**The strongest lever being least enforced (tension 1) dissolves once you stop conflating layers.** The linter is for the author; the test is for the runtime; prose is for the cooperative reader. The "tension" only exists if you expect a green linter to mean runtime safety. So the resolution is a paragraph, not a feature: state the layering explicitly in the grammar — `do-not` persuades a cooperative reader, `invariant` closes the gaps in that persuasion, `enforced-by` is the only thing that binds an uncooperative one — and say plainly that a clean linter run is *authoring hygiene, not a safety guarantee*. That asymmetry (cheap-and-always-appropriate thing is required; strong-but-not-always-warranted thing is recommended) is actually correct. You can't conjure tests, so requiring the strong lever would just produce fake `enforced-by` links. Document the layering, accept the asymmetry, done.

**The invariant-decaying-into-ceremony tension (3) I'd resolve by explicitly refusing to fix it.** The only check that catches "this invariant just paraphrases the do-not" is semantic, which is the exact thing the project correctly won't build. A lazy generalization is still marginally better than none, and the people writing SAFETY blocks are the ones who care. So the honest resolution is: leave it, and don't pretend a linter could catch it. Writing "we considered a check here and rejected it as unbuildable-deterministically" into the design notes is more valuable than any machinery — it stops a future handoff from re-litigating it.

**The `enforced-by` false-confidence tension (2) is the one real opportunity, and I'd stage it.** The sharp edge is that once a `[[test:...]]` *resolves to a real file*, nothing distinguishes "this guardrail is genuinely bound" from "someone wrote an aspirational link." You can't deterministically check the test *tests the right thing* (semantic — skip). But you can, eventually, check the cheap structural fact: does the named test exist and is it collected by the runner? That needs the repo's test layout — i.e. the same resolver that's already deferred for access-paths — so it's a defer, not a now. The in-spirit move available today is purely *positive and read-only*: when the `[[test:...]]` resolves, surface it in `atra-map`'s safety view as "bound," so the *verified* state is visually distinct from the *claimed* state. No finding, no nag, just making confidence track reality. But I'd gate even that on someone actually using `enforced-by` — otherwise it's a renderer for an empty set.

**Accumulation and the unverified foundation (4 and 5) are the same problem, and I'd resolve them with one move: an evidence gate written into AGENTS.md.** A stop-rule — *no new guardrail-authoring field without (a) a concrete failure the existing fields demonstrably couldn't have prevented, and (b) battery evidence that the authoring layer changes agent behavior at all.* That single rule does double duty: it caps the field creep (every new field now has to clear a real bar, not just a plausible anecdote), and it forces the §3 battery onto the critical path instead of leaving it perpetually "built, not run." The honest reading is that the last three handoffs were each individually justified but collectively built sophistication on an assumption nobody's tested. The gate converts "we keep adding because each addition sounds good" into "we add when we've shown the last addition worked."

So: two paragraphs of grammar/AGENTS honesty (layering + stop-rule), one deliberate non-fix documented as such, and one genuinely useful feature (`enforced-by` resolution → map's safety view) deferred behind actual usage. The throughline is that four of the five "fixes" are restraint, and the discipline that made these changes good — refusing semantic checks, refusing noisy findings — is the same discipline that says *the resolution to most of these is to write words, not code, and go run the test you already built.*"
